Great Migration
Summary
The great migration was an event where many people of different ethnic backgrounds came to what is now known as Canada. From 1915 to 1850, over 800000 people immigrated to what would be known as canada today.
Impacts
Without the great migration, Canada would not be such a diverse multicultural country as it is today.
The immense amount of people also means more support and better infrastructure; you can’t have a country with no people.
It most likely sped up the colonization of BNA.
Underground Railroad
Summary
The Underground railroad was led by a network of abolitionists. It was the largest anti-slavery movement in North America. An estimated 30 000 to 40 000 slaves were brought to Canada.
Impact
It saved a lot of lives and made many more people's lives better.
Symbolic in showing the signature Canadian culture and norms: kindness and empathy.
Red River Colony
Summary
The Red River colony was a multi-racial male dominant society near Assiniboine River, now known as Manitoba. In this colony, many men had indigenous spouses, and the oppression of women, brought from British laws, was imminent.
Impact
In this region, there were better relationships between the indigenous and the Europeans.

Britain's Withdrawal of Troops
Summary
Britain withdrew troops from BNA because of the Crimean War of 1853. They had a shortage of troops and desperately needed some.
Impacts
The colonies realized that they would need to be more independent.
Could have realized they might be able to rely on Britain and other countries in Europe.
The Trent Affair
Summary
The Trent affair was a diplomatic crisis between Britain and the USA that happened during the American civil war. The USA had illegally captured two Confederate convoys, who were released.
Impact
This put Canada in danger of being a battleground for the war between the two countries.
More cooperation between the colonies; at this point, they probably realized they would have to get along together.
Fenian Raids
Summary
These raids were launched by a secret society of Irish patriots who emigrated to the USA. They launched a few attacks on Canada, all of which were dealt with by the government successfully.
Impact
The attacks were multi-faced, meaning it would require coordination by each of the colonies to defend against properly.
St. Albans Raid
Summary
The St Albans Raid was carried out by 21 soldiers from Canada, who raided banks in St. Albans, Vermont, to “raise money”. In total, they took around $208,000 (US$ 3,400,000 in 2020).
Impact
Created more tensions along the USA/Canada border.
Threat of USA Annexation
Summary
At this time, BNA’s economy was weak. Each colony had much debt and was poorly structured. Their ability to defend an attack was very poor. At this time, Manifest Destiny, which stated the USA would take over all of North America, was popular.
Impact
Manifest destiny made colonies realize they would need to get together, although it didn’t happen at the time.

Charlottetown, Quebec and London Conferences
Summary
During the Charlottetown and Quebec conferences, fathers of confederation discussed the 72 resolutions that were the basis of Canada’s constitution, which eventually turned into the BNA act. During the London conference, the final details of Confederation were agreed on. A few months after the conferences, a bill was sent to the house of lords. The bill was accepted with little debate.
Impact
Set the confederation into motion.
Colonies came upon agreements.
Made Canada an official country.
Colonies were united.
Representation by Population
Summary
Representation by population, also called rep by pop, was the idea that a greater population should have a greater voice. This meant more seats in more populous cities. There was controversy as the population shifted from Canada East to Canada West.
Impacts
It was clear everyone needed a compromise for confederation, so the government was split into two parts: the upper and lower house. The upper house was not represented by the population, while the lower house was.
References
Excluded Indigenous Voices
Summary
During the confederation, Indigenous groups were not invited to the conferences, yet they were the ones so helpful to the early settlers. In Newfoundland and Labrador, the Indigenous groups were completely left out. They were not even mentioned once in the Terms of Union when Newfoundland and Labrador joined the confederation. It was not until 5 years later when Ottawa created a plan that gave them some funds. The first nations in the region still lacked the basic services other groups had in other provinces.
Impact
The indigenous felt omission, and not being invited to talk about confederation slowed down efforts to preserve culture, settle land claims and obtain self-government.
Residential schools and lost land still affects indigenous today.
The first nations had a huge impact on the colonization of Canada. They were kind enough to help the strangers that would soon take all their land.

Corn Laws
Summary
The British put tariffs regarding imports of wheat that were not from the colonies in BNA. They eventually lifted these laws as many “free traders” protested and Britain's demand for wheat increased.
Impact
Colonies were protected from competition from other countries.
As these laws were lifted, other countries had better competing grounds with the colonies.
Reciprocity Treaties
Summary
The reciprocity treaties introduced reduced tariffs between Canada and the USA. This was soon replaced by greater tariffs from the USA as fear over the annexation settled in. In 1994, the NAFTA agreement brought free to trade back between the USA, Canada, and Mexico.
Impact
The first treaty strengthened the bond between the USA and Canada. It was a mutual agreement that benefited both countries. Although it was removed in 1878, it was a step towards free trade.
Trading Posts
Summary
Many trading posts were set up in a 3.9 million sq. km land owned by the Hudson’s Bay Company called Rupert's land. This land was ideal for hunting, and many hunted for beaver pelts.
Impact
It was sold for 1.5 million dollars (around 50 million today) to the Canadian government after confederation: increasing the amount of land owned.
The fur trade also reinforced the relationships between the colonies.
